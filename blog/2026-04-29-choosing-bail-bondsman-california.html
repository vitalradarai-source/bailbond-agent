Step 1: Verify the CDI License Number Yourself

Every California bail bondsman must be licensed by the California Department of Insurance (CDI). This is non-negotiable — operating without a CDI license is a criminal offense under California Insurance Code §1800. Ask any bondsman for their license number. Then verify it yourself at insurance.ca.gov → "License Status" → search by name or number. Angels Bail Bonds operates under license #1K06080.

A legitimate bondsman volunteers their license number before you ask. If a bondsman cannot or will not provide their number, walk away. If they provide a number that does not check out at insurance.ca.gov, walk away.

Step 2: Confirm the 10% Premium — Anything Less Is a Trick

Under California Insurance Code §1800.4, the bail bond premium is fixed at 10% of the bail amount for licensed bondsmen. Period. There is no legal way to charge less for the actual fee.

You will see ads for "5% bail bonds" or "3% bail bonds" — these are misleading. The advertised number is typically the down payment on a payment plan, not the actual premium. The total fee is still 10%. A bondsman who quotes anything below 10% as the actual premium is either operating illegally or using deceptive marketing.

Red flag: Any bondsman quoting a premium below 10% as the actual fee. Walk away.

Step 3: Demand a Written Indemnity Agreement

The indemnity agreement spells out your financial liability if the defendant misses court, what triggers forfeiture, what collateral the bondsman can claim, and your obligations as cosigner. Read it carefully before signing. Ask questions about anything unclear.

A bondsman who pressures you to sign without reading is hiding something. Reputable bondsmen welcome questions, walk you through the agreement line by line, and never use urgency as a manipulation tool.

Step 4: Test 24/7 Availability Before You Need It

Arrests happen at 2am. The right bondsman answers the phone — not voicemail, not a service that takes a message. Verify before the emergency: call the number from their website at an off hour and see who answers. If you reach a real licensed agent who can answer questions about California bail procedure, that is a green flag.

Step 5: Check Years in Business and Local Experience

A bondsman who has been licensed for 5+ years has handled the situations that test newcomers — forfeiture proceedings, defendant relocations, complex collateral disputes, ICE detainer cases. Angels Bail Bonds has been licensed since 1958 — that is decades of California bail experience across LA County, Orange County, San Diego, the Bay Area, and beyond.

Local experience matters when minutes count. A bondsman who knows the booking procedures at the specific jail your loved one is in (LASD, OCSD, SDSD, San Francisco County, Alameda County) moves faster than one calling that county for the first time.

Step 6: Confirm Transparent Payment Plan Terms

Most legitimate California bondsmen offer payment plans that spread the 10% premium over weeks or months. Ask about: down payment percentage, payment schedule, late-fee policy, and whether interest is charged. Under California Department of Insurance regulations, interest on the bond premium itself is restricted. Get all terms in writing before signing.

Step 7: Confirm Local Reputation

Check Google reviews, Better Business Bureau rating, and ask the criminal defense attorney (if you have one) who they have seen handle bonds in your county. Reputable bondsmen have years of accumulated reviews and a clean BBB record. New operations with no review history or recent BBB complaints warrant extra caution.

The 7 Red Flags That Should Make You Walk Away

  • Refusing to provide a CDI license number, or providing one that does not verify at insurance.ca.gov
  • Cash only, no receipt
  • Premium quoted under 10% as the actual fee
  • No written indemnity agreement, or pressure to sign without reading
  • No physical office address
  • High-pressure sales: "sign now or your loved one stays in jail" — this is manipulation
  • Payment requested via gift cards, cryptocurrency, or wire transfer to a personal account

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I verify a California bail bondsman is licensed?
Verify at insurance.ca.gov → License Status → search by name or license number. Angels Bail Bonds: #1K06080. If a bondsman cannot or will not provide a number, walk away.
Why is the premium 10%?
California Insurance Code §1800.4 fixes the premium at 10% of bail for licensed bondsmen. Anyone advertising less as the actual rate is illegal or deceptive.
What red flags should I watch for?
No license number, cash-only with no receipt, premiums under 10%, no written agreement, high-pressure sales, no physical office, payment via gift cards or crypto.
What documents do I need?
Cosigner: photo ID, proof of address, proof of income. Defendant: full legal name, DOB, booking number. Higher bail may need collateral.
Can a bondsman refuse my case?
Yes — they evaluate flight risk and financial exposure. A declining bondsman is being honest about risk.
